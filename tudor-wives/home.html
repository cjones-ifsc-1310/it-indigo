All About Old King Harry, Himself

Henry Tudor VIII was the second son of Henry Tudor VII and Elizabeth of York, the union that healed England after the ceaseless civil wars throughout medieval times: the War of the Roses. Since he was the second born son, his father, the king, originally intended Henry (affectionately called Harry by family) to become a Cardinal in the Catholic Church to serve England and be a support to his elder brother, Arthur, Prince of Wales, the future king of England. However, Arthur unexpectedly died in 1502, leaving Henry as the sole male heir to England's throne, to which he would ascend in 1509 at the age of 17.

What did Henry VIII Really Look Like?

At the age of 17, the now King Henry was lauded across the world as "the most handsome prince in Christendom," standing at over six feet tall, and described as slim, toned, and strong due to his prowess at all things athletic (jousting, wrestling, tennis, etc.). His complexion was said to be "fair" with "cheeks like twin roses." He is also described as having small blue eyes and reddish-gold hair.

Henry's Illness and Decline

But, did Henry's leg wound lead to his budding tyrannical behavior? Let's be honest. We already know he was mysoginistic, and as a prince, he was probably already spoiled and self-obsessed. Some historians believe that the jousting injury, which caused a concussion and the king to lie unconcious for more than two hours, was actually a brain injury that began to change Henry's temperment.

Early Years

In his early years, while his brother was sent to Wales to learn the art of ruling, Henry was allowed a more moderate upbringing until the age of ten, when his brother suddenly died, making Henry the sole male heir. At that point, his father became obsessed with over-protecting him, and he was rarely seen without a chaperone guarding his every step. Henry chafed over his father's restrictions, but even so, he became a star pupil in the classroom, excelling at languages, music, history, writing poetry, and philisophy. By the time Henry ascended the throne in 1509, he felt ready for the challenge and he was England's greatest hope for a peaceful and lucrative reign.

In Henry VII's final year, Henry disclosed that he wanted to marry his late brother's widow, Catherine of Aragon, and was denied for several reasons. The biggest reason is because at that time, it was believed that God would punish a man who would marry his brother's wife if the marriage had been consummated. However, Catherine and Henry applied for a dispensation from the Pope to allow them to marry, Catherine having sworn that Arthur had been too ill to consummate the marriage. Because Arthur had died shortly after marrying Catherine, she was believed, therefore clearing them from the legal implications of affinity.

And that, ladies and gentlemen, is where our tale picks up!

Supreme Leader

Once upon a time, Henry the VIII had earned the title "Defender of the Faith" by Pope Leo X for writing a pamphlet damning Lutheran rhetoric, which was spreading throughout England at the time. However, due to the Pope's reluctance to rule on the "King's Great Matter" and Anne Boleyn's influence, Henry began to break away from Catholic customs. Anne had convinced him that he was created to be king by God and that no one on Earth should have dominion over him. Thus, enter the Church of England and the beginning of Protestantism.

Henry's Sad End

After six wives and fathering three living children, Henry VIII ultimately died alone. Between a mysterious illness, his leg ulcer, and his 400 pound weight gain, Henry had secluded himself in one of his palaces, with only his most trusted advisors gathered around him. Unfortunately, since it was then an executable offense to predict the monarch's death, his advisors did not tell him until the very end that he was dying. Henry's dead body then laid for two days before his advisors, many forming a faction to prevent the current Queen Consort, Catherine Parr, from having any contact with or any influence over the new young King Edward VI.
